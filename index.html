Executive Summary (What the market is doing to Cook)

Bloomington is a tight labor market operating inside a manufacturing labor cluster. The talent base exists (high assembler concentration), but “available” labor is limited (12-month unemployment average ~3.56%). A postings snapshot (Feb 20, 2026; 20 miles; within 30 days) shows broad competition for production and logistics-adjacent roles raising the importance of offer competitiveness, speed-to-hire, and retention.

Risk: low unemployment → time-to-fill Risk: warehouse/forklift demand competes for the same workforce Risk: maintenance & machining are bottleneck roles Opportunity: internal ladders (assembler → inspection/QA)
  • Primary battleground: $18-$22/hour roles (assemblers, movers, warehouse/forklift)
  • Structural advantage: skills-aligned labor pool (high assembler and inspection concentration)
  • Structural constraint: tight labor + high employer competition reduces “ready supply” even with a sizable workforce base

Housing Band Insights (Retention Friction)

Housing acts like a “shadow wage.” If rent rises, hourly wage expectations and churn pressure typically follow (JP Morgan 2025).
Data: U.S. Census Bureau Monroe County ACS 2020-2024 5-year estimates. “Affordability wage” converts monthly housing cost into an hourly wage benchmark.

Affordability Method (transparent)

Assumptions: 40 hrs/week, 52 weeks/year (= 2,080 hrs), housing share rule (cost of living is 30% or 35% of gross income)
Hourly wage = (Monthly housing cost × 12) ÷ (Rule × 2,080). Dual income divides required income across two full-time earners.

Scenario Rent Wage Mortgage Wage Interpretation
30% Rule (single earner) $22.94/hr $29.46/hr Conservative affordability benchmark
35% Rule (single earner) $19.66/hr $25.25/hr Less conservative; reflects higher burden tolerance
Dual income (two earners) $11.47/hr each $14.73/hr each Shows why household composition changes “affordability” reality
  • Key takeaway: assembler median (~$17.69/hr) is below both the 30% (~$22.94) and 35% (~$19.66) rent thresholds and meaningfully below the mortgage thresholds
  • Retention Implication: if Cook hires at the low end of the $18–$22 band, housing can amplify churn pressure unless total rewards (benefits, attendance bonuses, reliable hours, etc.) close the gap

PESTLE Diagnosis (Bloomington)

Political / Legal

  • Indiana’s labor relations baseline (Right-to-Work) shapes environment and expectations
  • Watch: incentives and development policies that accelerate competitor expansions (labor demand shocks)

Economic

  • Tight labor (~3.56% avg unemployment) increases vacancy cost; retention ROI is unusually high
  • Wage growth in assembler/supervisor benchmarks indicates rising expectations and compression risk

Social / Demographic

  • Bloomington is an institution-heavy market whcih shapes benefit expectations and job-switching patterns
  • Watch: labor force participation and “stable workforce” availability (beyond student seasonality)

Technological

  • Opportunity: internal ladders + rapid training for entry roles; move talent into inspection/QA and lead roles
  • Watch: competitor automation investments shifting demand toward maintenance/tech

Environmental

  • Commute reliability affects hourly attendance; labor shed resilience matters for shift staffing
  • Watch: seasonal disruption and commuting radius constraints

Institutional / Ecosystem

  • Skills-aligned manufacturing cluster supports hiring but heightens competition
  • Watch: temp agency activity and logistics hiring waves (major competitor channel for hourly labor)

Recruitment & Retention Recommendations (Bloomington)

  • Compete on speed: reduce time-to-offer for entry roles; consider same-day conditional offers.
  • Win the $18-$22 band: align entry pay to assembler/forklift benchmarks; if below, offset via total rewards + predictable hours.
  • Build a visible ladder: formalize (assembler → inspector/QA → lead → supervisor) and communicate it at hiring.
  • Protect bottlenecks: maintenance/machining require proactive retention and pipelines (small base, high impact).
  • Address housing friction: consider shift premiums, transport support, or retention bonuses to reduce churn pressure.
